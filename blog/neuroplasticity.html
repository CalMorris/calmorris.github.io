Neuroplasticity & Mindset

What is neuroplasticity and why does it matter?

Neuroplasticity is the brains adaptation to internal or external stimuli by re-organizing the structure and functions of neural connections. This means that the bonds between neurons where information is relayed, become stronger and allow for better long term memory and motor function.

The term neuroplasticity or brain plasticity, was defined because brain is malleable like plastic and can transform, allowing the learning of new skills both physical and mental.

Because our nervous system is malleable, it has allowed us to learn physical skills such as; walking, running and jumping. Mental skills such as knowledge of a subject and self-awareness so we can adapt to our environment. Our brains are always changing regardless of age.

This is beneficial as it means a person can learn and improve on skills through practice and visualisation. Practice strengthens the bonds between neurons and allows for better performance of a skill.

In addition, learning new skills alters the brain in a way that transforms the neural networks. The more often a person is learning new skills, the better they become at learning because they have strengthened their neural network and primed the brain to learn new skills more readily.

What can I do to benefit from neuroplasticity?

• Learn about new skills often to improve learning ability.

• Practice regularly to strengthen neural pathways. The more you practice, the stronger connections become.

• Put information in context. Information that is shared in a way where relationships are clear, will generate greater brain cell activity and will achieve more successful long-term memory.

• Set goals that are slightly above current knowledge and capabilities.

Growth Mindset: what is it & why is it relevant?

One of the most basic beliefs we carry about ourselves, are to do with our belief in our ability.

A “fixed mindset” assumes that our character, intelligence, and creative ability are static which we can’t change in any meaningful way, and success is the affirmation of that inherent intelligence, an assessment of how those measure up against an equally fixed standard; striving for success and avoiding failure at all costs become a way of maintaining the sense of being smart or skilled.

A “growth mindset,” on the other hand, thrives on challenge and sees failure not as evidence of unintelligence but as a heartening springboard for growth and for stretching our existing abilities.

For example, a fixed mindset will view learning to play the piano as a challenge and when faced with possible failure, give up easily, avoiding the challenge. This will feel like a pointless skill and ignore and feedback that may be helpful to success.

A growth mindset will embrace the challenge and persist when facing setbacks. Effort and feedback is part of the path to mastery. The success of others is something that can be learned from.

This is relevant to learning any skill because mindset plays a large part in learning. If mistakes are viewed as a negative consequence instead of a learning opportunity, growth is much slower and the probability of giving up is greater.

Enabling a growth mindset at EDA

After reviewing fixed vs growth mindsets, I now recognize I have had a fixed mindset at times and that has limited opportunities for myself. This is a skill that needs to be worked on, especially when learning becomes more challenging. The following strategies are now incorperated into my learning plan while attending EDA:

• Embrace challenge and acknowledge failure is part of the process and a learning opportunity.

• Reflect regularly on the process and journal lessons learned.

• Read work of successful people.

• Practice and place an emphisis on positive effort while tasked with a challenge.
